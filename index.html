Socioeconomic Development

Frequent and rapid spatially explicit assessment of socioeconomic development is critical for achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) at both national and global levels. Over the past decades, scientists have proposed many methods for estimating human activity on the Earth’s surface at various spatiotemporal scales using Defense Meteorological Satellite Program Operational Line System (DMSP-OLS) and Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite (VIIRS) nighttime light (NTL) data. However, the NTL data and the associated processing methods have limited their reliability and applicability for systematic measuring and mapping of socioeconomic development. Our assessments show that our nighttime light models can produce accurate subnational GDP data products for mapping and monitoring human development uniformly across the globe.

Learning Outcome Assessment

I have collaborated with many professors to develop assessment instruments that could provide evidence that graduating Geography and Environmental Science majors possessed more skills and knowledge and confidence in their skills and knowledge than they did as incoming first year students. The instruments we developed for learning outcomes assessment are described and presented. Qualitative and statistical analysis of several years of data demonstrate statistically significant improvements in the objective quizzes and self-assessments of the graduating students. The results provided a satisfying body of evidence suggesting that the teaching and learning taking place in our department is effective while also identifying some issues we need to address. This data provides a mechanism for the faculty to reflect on our curriculum and teaching practices to identify ways to improve them. These instruments are used on an on-going basis to inform departmental program reviews, inquiries from accreditation teams, and to promote the department within the University.

Education Psychology

Over the past decades, the concept of self-regulated learning has been well studied by theoriticans with various emphasis and perspectives. From the social cognitive perspective, many researchers have indicated that self-regulated learning involves a number of components in interaction. Although theoretical background and empirical evidence have both shown that academic self-efficacy is a significant predictor of academic performance, little is known regarding the specific mechanisms to explain this relationship between motivational attributes, self-efficacy, and their interaction effects on academic performances. Therefore, I have conducted systematic review of current empirical studies to extrapolate the mechanism to indicate how ones’ cognition direct their behaviors to influences learning outcomes. In addition, I have also collaborated with researchers from different institutions to conduct best-evidence synthesis to review the past 20 years of rigorous reading intervention research to identify effective programs of instruction for Grade K–3 English Learners (ELs), as well as to determine the average effect of reading instruction on reading outcomes for this population.
